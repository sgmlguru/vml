Comments from Reviewer

[I am a little worried that] the authors may be bombarded by a series of "this has already been done" from the audience.

Potential Improvments

The abstract of the paper "Multilevel versions" presents a very interesting challenge (versioning on top of an exiting non-XML aware VCS). However the paper disappoints because it fails to:

  • explain and discuss the problem,

  • show an appropriate solution,

  • discuss adequately the pros and cons of that solution.

I make the following suggestions:

  • The paper could use a better sorganization. The reader is dragged back and forth through different branches of the discussion without a clear path: it is hard to understand where the discussion is coming from and where it is going.

  • There is also some confusion with the terminology used. For example, I have never seen the term "version handled [content]", that I assume be identical in meaning to the more familiar "versioned [content]".

  • This paper does not demonstrate knowledge of the existing theory of versioning and of the current VCS tools. This is reflected on the very poor bibliography. Now knowing the past experiences is not a very big problem: having a "beginner's mind" can surely lead to interesting new discoveries and advancement of the art. [That said,] it looks like the author has some experience, and some scars, in the field of working with versioned documents. I suggest the author have a look at the relevant tools and bibliography so that their experiences can be described more effectively, addressing still open problems (the translation problem is still very open) or reframing old problems in terms of new technologies.

    [What it looks like to me is that] in this case, the author ended up rediscovering the gist of modern distributed version control system such as git or mercurial. For example, the three "branches" that the paper describes are, adopting the familiar git notation, the "repository copy", the "index/staged copy" and the "work area copy" of a document. Similarly, part of the version map is equivalent to the "index file" of git. Last, "use latest problem" is the problem behind "floating submodules" vs "fixed submodules". I suggest the author to have a look at all of these topics, maybe starting from a book such as ProGit (CC BY-NC-SA) and then proceeding to scholarly literature.

  • A special notion is needed for the fact that they author also addresses the issue of "translation" in VCS. Translations are just one case of the more general problem of dealing with the consistency of related-but-different files in VCS, another common case is having files with the same content but in different formats. Some early work on these matters can be read in the Akoma Ntoso URI scheme (or naming convention) [http://www.akomantoso.org/release-notes/akoma-ntoso-3.0-schema/naming-conventions-1]. Akoma Ntoso is an XML vocabulary for legal documents. In places like the EU the same act is translated in various languages and there is a file of each of these translations. In additions these laws are changed every now and then, so there is a versioning system in place to refer to "Act 10" in whatever version it is currently, "Act 10" as it was in 2010 or "Act 10" as it would be if "Proposition 9" passes tomorrow. All that while dealing with multiple formats and multiple languages.

  • A last remark about terminology: the author writes "implying a versioning logic well-known from software development". I admit am I not familiar with the versioning scheme shown by the author. The only schemes I know are

    • Even/odd: Major versions are for big releases, odd minor versions are development version, even minor versions are stable versions with fixes: 1.0 is stable, 1.1 is development, 1.2 is stable, 1.2.1 is development.

    • Major/Minor + suffix: Major versions are for big releases, minor versions are for stable releases with small fixes or minor features. The development releases have suffixes such as "a/b" (alpha/beta), "-rc" (release candidate), "-dev", etc.

Comments from Reviewer

Potential Improvements

Nice clear discussion of the problem in the section "The 'use latest' problem".

  • Under "Save, Check In, Check Out" you write "Stable resources can be checked out to Stage 1 to be edited, however, in which case they are locked in Stable while awaiting a check-in from Stage 1." I think you are re-creating a problem known in conventional version management systems since RCS: RCS allowed one to check out a file with a lock, which prevented others from updating the document until the same user either checked in a new version or explicitly released the lock. One of the reasons CVS doesn't follow this model is that a survey of some sites using RCS showed that the large majority of write locks were held by people who had left the organization and were no longer available to clear the locks. (Locking also didn't prevent the lost-update problem in RCS, leading to the automatic merging behavior of CVS and SVN.) This is to some extent (exclusively?) a social problem, not a technical one, but it's significant. If you introduce locking, you must also specify in the design what happens when the lock is never cleared.

  • I suspect that the sites surveyed were academic sites, not commercial sites with well developed workflows. But even in a well developed workflow, the question will arise: what happens if the lock is never released?

  • It would be helpful to have a concrete example of the urn scheme in the section "Version abstraction", just to help reassure the reader that they understand your scheme. (And there! you see? I didn't. You said four positions for language and country, and I said 'well, what about a hyphen?' because 'en-GB' has five characters by my count. But I see that you aren't counting the invariant hyphen.)

  • As you observe, the resource structure you define is a nice recursive version structure. Since you just said quite clearly that you were only having two levels, this reader is now asking "does he not notice that his design doesn't need recursion and indeed cannot usefully exploit recursion? Or is he setting things up for a more complex multi-level design to come?"

  • In note 8, for "lose track of which language and version that contains the latest information" read either "lose track of the language ... that contains the latest information" or "lose track of which ... version contains ...".

  • The use of xml:lang in the document skeleton under 'Version mapping with translations' is strictly speaking tag abuse, I think: xml:lang describes the language of the content of the element (here: the URNs), not the content of the thing(s) a hyperlink points at. My advice to you, as a language lawyer, is to rename the attribute 'language' or something like that. Or flash a winning smile to disarm the audience and say "Of course, this is tag abuse, but this is how we do it."
